What is an Exoplanet and how are they identified?

Simply put, an exoplanet is a planet that orbits a star outside our solar system. Exoplanets were theorized throughout the 20th century, but observing planets outside our solar system was not possible with the technology of the time. It wasn't until the Hubble Space Telescope was launched in the early 1990s that the first exoplanet discoveries were made.

There are a number of methods for identifying exoplanet candidates, and the relative importance of these methods has changed over time. These include transit, radial velocity, direct imaging, gravitational microlensing, and astrometry. For more information on exoplanet discovery methods, see here. Transit, Lightcurves, and Threshold Crossing Events

Transit is a exoplanet discovery methodology that involves observing the light emanating from a nearby star. When observing a star from earth, an orbiting planet may transition in front of the star, creating a dimming effect on the light that reaches earth. In this way, the observed light from a star can indicate the presense of planets outside our solar system.

Data on the light emanating from nearby stars are collected from large telescopes. The largest depositories of light data on individual stars come from the Kepler Space Telescope or the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS). The light observed from each star is plotted over time to create a star's light curve.

Astronomers study individual stars' light curves - taking note of patterns and changes in the brightness of the light observed over time - in order to identify threshold crossing events (TCEs). Below is an example light curve from the star Kepler 90-G, and shows the impact of a transiting planet on the brightness of the light observed from earth. The pattern shown in the light curve is considered a TCE since the change in the brightness suggests an object has passed in between the star and the observing telescope on earth.

But how does one distinguish between a planet passing in front of a star versus any number of other phenomenon that could dim the light observed from earth?

A TCE can be explained by a myriad of different factors other than transit, and light curves don't always provide a clean indication of the presence of an exoplanet. For instance, the pattern in the light curve below, which is distinct from Kepler 90-G above, has not been classified as a TCE.
